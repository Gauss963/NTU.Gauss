A Guide to Driving Around Taipei

Posted on 2021-12-12 · English version of a Chinese essay

If driving with no destination in mind is a way of stepping outside ordinary life for a while, then in Taipei the right roads and the right hour make all the difference.

On the Matter of Driving for Its Own Sake

Once I am in the driver's seat, the city's network of roads seems to remove the usual limits from life. The destination no longer matters. What matters is the process of moving forward, as if motion itself were a form of pursuit, something not unlike searching for truth. I like driving around whether I am in a good mood or a bad one. There is something in following the road with no visible end, slipping into the rhythm of traffic, and leaving behind for a while whatever anxieties or pressures have been pressing in from every direction. Since driving for pleasure is such a casual and impulsive thing, does anyone really need a guide to it? In Taipei, I think the answer is yes. Unless you genuinely enjoy studying every red light one by one, or find great pleasure in staring at the rear of the car ahead of you, a guide may actually help.

Choosing the Right Time

Timing matters a great deal. Rush hour is obviously the worst possible moment. Heavy traffic occupies nearly every inch of the road, and your right foot spends all its time moving between the accelerator and the brake. At that point, you are not driving for pleasure so much as training your patience. The times I would recommend are early morning, late afternoon, and anything after 11:00 p.m. Of those, the one I like best and choose most often is the late-night window after eleven.

Choosing the Route

Taipei is a city of connections. Within it you have highways, expressways, and urban arterial roads built for speed. The least suitable of these, in my view, are the highways. Traffic jams aside, highway speeds themselves make it difficult to truly relax. That alone is enough to disqualify them as pleasure-driving routes. Once you remove highways from consideration, what Taipei offers in abundance are urban express roads. Though they are often casually called expressways, in practice they still follow the design logic and legal framework of ordinary roads, which also means that speeding fines tend to be a little less brutal.

Below are the roads in Taipei that I think are genuinely suited to driving for the sake of driving.

  • Civic Boulevard Viaduct
  • Huandong Boulevard
  • Xinsheng Viaduct
  • Huanhe North-South Expressway
  • Shuiyuan Expressway
  • National Freeway No. 3A
  • Dunhua South and North Roads
  • Ren'ai Road

Route 1: Civic Boulevard Viaduct to Huandong Boulevard

Civic Boulevard is the central east-west artery of downtown Taipei. It cuts straight across the city center, and because the viaduct rises so high above street level, it offers an unusually open view of the city. If what you want is urban scenery, this is the route.

Huandong Boulevard begins at the intersection of Bade Road and Keelung Road, effectively where Civic Boulevard ends, and runs east to Nangang Exhibition Center. Every time I drive it, I find myself thinking the asphalt quality is poor. There is loose gravel on the road, and it is hardly kind to a car's paintwork. But in its later stretch, the stacked double-deck viaduct gives off that unmistakable feeling of urban progress. Many car-review programs even use it as a filming location.

  • Suggested approach: Enter from the Dadaocheng ramp onto the Civic Boulevard Viaduct, then continue onto Huandong Boulevard.
  • Suggested pace: Fast, for the city view.
  • Recommendation: ★★★☆☆

Route 2: Xinsheng Viaduct, Huanhe North-South Expressway, Shuiyuan Expressway

Xinsheng Viaduct was built in 1984, so it belongs to an older generation of elevated roads. But its straight geometry, relatively small number of ramps, and above all the absence of endless speed cameras make it one of the most comfortable roads in Taipei.

The Huanhe North-South Expressway together with the Shuiyuan Expressway is probably my favorite route in the city. Taking the Shuiyuan Road area near Shida as a dividing line, the road to the south becomes Shuiyuan Expressway, and to the north it becomes Huanhe. The route follows the river, and because of the levees and the bridges that cross the water, the road rises and falls in a lively rhythm. You get river scenery, but also something close to the feeling of riding a roller coaster.

  • Suggested approach: Enter Xinsheng Viaduct from the Jinan Road ramp, drive to the end, turn left onto Jiantan Road, then left onto Chengde Road. After crossing Chengde Bridge, turn right onto Dunhuang Road, continue to the end, and turn left into Section 2 of Huanhe North Road, which leads directly to Shuiyuan Expressway. After passing the Jingmei ramp, you can step on the accelerator for a stretch, though safety should come first.
  • Suggested pace: Medium. There are quite a few speed cameras later on.
  • Caution: Shuiyuan Expressway has a sharp curve where excessive speed can be dangerous.
  • Recommendation: ★★★★★

Route 3: National Freeway No. 3A

This road is formally part of the national freeway network, and at present it is also the only freeway section open to large-displacement motorcycles. It connects Xinhai Road to the Muzha area and includes two tunnels along the way. The scenery is ordinary, and there is nothing especially distinctive about it. It makes the list mainly because it is very close to NTU.

  • Suggested approach: Enter from Xinhai Road, exit at the Wanfang interchange, take a short break on Xinguang Road in front of the Taipei Zoo, then return by the same route.
  • Suggested pace: Fast. Simple, direct, and convenient.
  • Recommendation: ★☆☆☆☆

Route 4: Dunhua South and North Roads

One of Taipei's classic ceremonial avenues. At night, when traffic thins out, driving in the fast lane feels almost luxurious. The raised pavement markers along the route reflect like runway lights, and the effect is genuinely pleasurable from behind the wheel.

  • Suggested approach: Enter the fast lane from Keelung Road, drive all the way to Songshan Airport, loop once inside, and then retrace the route back.
  • Suggested pace: Slow, elegant, and composed.
  • Recommendation: ★★★★☆

Route 5: Ren'ai Road

Another of Taipei's signature ceremonial roads. It passes through some of the city's most expensive residential districts and at its widest reaches a full hundred meters across, a broad tree-lined boulevard unlike almost any other. After midnight, when you can drive along the bus lane, it feels as though the sleeping city itself is quietly coming alive again.

  • Suggested approach: Enter from Ren'ai Circle and head west along the center of the road.
  • Suggested pace: Slow, elegant, and composed.
  • Recommendation: ★★★★☆

Conclusion

Some people who commute through Taipei every day may read all of this and think it is nonsense. But that is only because they have never gone out at the right time. They have never experienced these roads at the right hour, never really experienced the act of driving for its own sake, never really experienced the city in this way. On a night when you have nowhere you need to be, sit down in the driver's seat and see whether you can feel the pleasure of simply driving.
