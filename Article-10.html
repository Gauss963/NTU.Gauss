Let the Whole City be My Library

Posted on 2024-05-04 · English version of a Chinese essay

April downpours and May sunshine both make you want to stay somewhere air-conditioned. A few days ago, I happened to drive past the National Central Library and realized that it had been years since I last sat in its study room.

My years in study rooms almost perfectly overlap with my high school and college years. Each library now stands for a different chapter of that life.

Taipei Public Library Main Branch

The Taipei Public Library Main Branch is usually called the Main Library. I know that, for many people at NTU, "the Main Library" means the one at the end of Palm Boulevard. But for people from Taipei, or at least those from Zhongzheng and Da'an, "the Main Library" meant the Taipei Public Library Main Branch. At least that was true for me before I got into NTU. It faces Jianguo Elevated Road, with Daan Forest Park just across the street. The surroundings are pleasant, mostly residential, and in the evenings the study area fills up with middle school and high school students.

I had been going to the Taipei Public Library Main Branch for as long as I can remember. When I was little, I read in the children's section in the basement. A little older, I started browsing novels on the fourth and fifth floors. In high school, I moved to the study areas on the fourth, fifth, and sixth floors. The library was very close to Yanping High School, so after class a group of us would walk along Jianguo South Road to study there. On the way we passed hand-shaken drink shops, snack stalls, the Xinyi Lianqin development that was once sensationally said to be even more expensive than Ren'ai Di Bao, and the old Daan Public Housing complex with all its unmistakable sense of another era. On rainy days, we could even walk under Jianguo Elevated Road through the parking area and make it to the library without getting wet.

I certainly did not spend the time saved on commuting actually studying. Back then, seat assignment in the study room still worked through paper tickets, so there was basically no limit on how long you could be away from your seat. And because there were so many food options near the library, I often did not settle down to study until after seven in the evening. My most common meal was the pot stickers from the Bafang Dumpling shop across the alley. If I went there to study ten times, I probably ate there eight. One of the remaining times I would have knife-cut noodles, and the last one I would end up in 7-Eleven. For a while Bafang was under renovation, and I had no choice but to survive on convenience store food.

During that period, I was mostly studying high school English and math, especially math. After I had eaten enough, I would pull out that yellow-and-blue "interactive mathematics workbook." I always wrote in it with pencil, because I kept discovering mistakes and needed to revise things. Our group would discuss problems whenever we got stuck, but sometimes a single question would eat up half an hour. In the end, I was not especially efficient there.

And inefficiency was not my only problem. I also had a habit of taking breaks after barely half an hour. The stairwell in the library had huge floor-to-ceiling windows. You could watch the traffic on Jianguo Elevated Road, and at night even Daan Forest Park seemed strangely irresistible when set against an open textbook. More than once, I went home having written less than a page of math.

Before closing each night, the Taipei Public Library used to play a song called "Falling in Love with Taipei Library." It was not exactly beautiful, but it was familiar, and its awkward melody always told everyone that it was time to end the day's labor and go home.

National Central Library

The National Central Library, usually shortened to "the National Library," is the most important library in the Republic of China, and its holdings are also the most academic. It stands directly across from Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall, surrounded by central government offices. Its study room is in the basement and open to anyone over sixteen, so you never see middle school students there. Because the place is perpetually occupied by nearby high school students and civil service exam candidates, it is almost always full no matter the season. Many people go there precisely to experience that charged atmosphere of serious study.

One day in my first year of high school, I discovered that the study room stayed open until ten at night. So on a weekend I went to the first floor, applied for a reader's card, and headed down to the basement. The books there cannot be borrowed, so it is not really a library card in the usual sense. After that, I became a regular. On weekdays I came depending on my mood, but on weekends I studied there almost without fail.

On school days, I would wait for the 263 bus, later renamed the Ren'ai Main Line, at the stops near Ren'ai and Jianguo or at Ren'ai Circle, get off at Jingfu Circle, and walk two minutes to the library. In the evening, the 263 crawled all the way down Ren'ai Road toward Taipei Main Station and was almost always packed. But Ren'ai Road may be the most relaxed thoroughfare in all of Taipei. Luxury homes line both sides, the median is wide enough to feel like it could hold a park, and dense greenery suggests a broad, unhurried boulevard. All of that stood in sharp contrast to the people inside the bus, crammed together on their way home from work or cram school.

There was essentially nowhere to eat near the National Library. The only reliable source of food was a single 7-Eleven, so going there to study meant resigning myself to microwaved meals and chilled rice balls. But food was not the real problem. Seats were. As I said, the study room was almost always full, regardless of the time of year. So even though Taipei Main Station, with all its food choices, was only one or two bus stops farther, I always had to hurry straight to the reservation machine and secure a seat for the evening.

Of course, there was no guarantee I would get one. Private schools finished about an hour and a half later than the public high schools near the library, and sometimes I arrived only to find every seat taken. On those days I would sit in the 7-Eleven dining area and eat slowly, hoping that by the time I finished, something would open up. Sometimes nothing did, and all I could do was start studying right there under the convenience store lights.

When I think of the National Library study room, I think of that purple booklet of past second-round Physics Olympiad problems. During Olympiad season, I spent at least half my time there working through it. I usually simulated the real exam and gave myself three hours to see how many points I could earn. One full set a day, plus review, easily consumed five hours. My second-round results actually got worse each year because I was also preparing for the college entrance exam, but even so, whenever the National Library study room comes to mind, that purple book is the first thing I see.

One advantage of studying there was the sunken plaza outside and Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall, or Liberty Square, just across the road. Whenever I felt I could not read another line, I would go wander around those spaces. The library allowed everyone an hour outside, and that was enough time for me to walk up to the terrace of the memorial hall and look out over the whole square.

To me, the defining feature of the National Library study room was how serious everyone was. I went back there specifically for this essay, just to remember how it had felt. It was the most oppressive and physically uncomfortable study environment I have ever known. At any given moment, it felt twice as intense as finals week in the NTU study rooms.

Fuhsing Private School Library

The Fuhsing library is not large, but it is beautiful. It sits at the very top of the school, with a triangular ceiling that rises through three stories and an entire wall of glass that fills the room with daylight. During the day, you can read there quite comfortably without turning on the lights at all.

During my senior year, anyone who stayed for evening study would go to the library on the eighth floor after dinner and remain there until 9:10. Naturally, what we studied was the material for the General Scholastic Ability Test. Since the school was not about to let us go out for dinner, everyone either ate a lunchbox from home or whatever was available in the basement cafeteria. Nobody liked the cafeteria food, and under the pressure of studying, many of us would go home and eat again afterward. One evening, a group of us sat chatting on the seventh-floor terrace before heading into the library and happened to run into the principal. He gave us scallion pancakes and pot stickers he had bought from the dumpling shop next door. They were nothing extraordinary, just ordinary pot stickers and pancakes, but I still remember exactly how they tasted.

Compared with the other places, the Fuhsing library carried real blood and tears. In my senior year, my first choice was NTU Physics, but to get in I needed at least a 14 in Chinese. So every evening, when I made my plan for what to study, Chinese was always on it. Every day I opened that dirty ochre-colored reference book on classical Chinese knowledge, flipped through pages full of markings in every color of pen, and tried to force the whole thing into my head.

That year I studied to the point of exhaustion. As the exam drew near, I put my phone away completely and eliminated every possible distraction. One evening after study hall, I walked to the bus stop as usual. Christmas was close, and the trees along Dunhua North and South Roads were wrapped in beautiful LED lights. I remember thinking how pretty they were, and then reaching for my wallet. That was when I realized I had not brought it. I had no phone either, so I could not call home. In the end, I decided to walk all the way back. The whole way, I kept looking at passing cars and hoping I might spot my parents by chance. Of course I never did. After more than forty minutes, I finally made it home. In that moment, all I could think was how badly I wanted the exam to be over. Once the exam was over, I would bring my phone with me again. National Taiwan University Main Library

The National Taiwan University Main Library, the Main Library in the vocabulary of NTU students, stands at the far end of Palm Boulevard.

I had a vivid impression of it long before I ever became an NTU student. When I was little, my parents used to take me wandering around campus, and I remember seeing a college student sitting outside the library using a MacBook back when the Apple logo still glowed. He looked impossibly intelligent. Oddly enough, the scene did not make me want to attend NTU. It made me want to become the sort of person who carried himself with ease, elegance, and composure.

In my first year, I was lucky enough to have a classmate from high school in the department. Once you know one person, meeting everyone else becomes much easier. I often went with him to study in the discussion rooms across from the basement study area, usually because of quizzes or midterms. I remember very clearly the first time I went to the study room. I simply needed somewhere to finish my first Applied Mechanics homework set, which must have had ten problems in it. I sat there for three or four hours with A4 paper and a ballpoint pen.

Looking back on all those years in study rooms, each stage had its own meaning. From middle school to university, from the Taipei Public Library Main Branch to the National Central Library, from the Fuhsing library to the NTU Main Library, these places witnessed my joys and frustrations alike. They remain an inseparable part of an awkward, earnest student life.

Now I have finally made it through those years of being beaten up by academic pressure. Once in a very long while, I still find myself back there in dreams, under the desk lamp from my exam-preparation days, or in that stairwell overlooking Jianguo Elevated Road. And yet I have never come to resent libraries. I still love that little world full of books, old people, and students preparing for exams.
