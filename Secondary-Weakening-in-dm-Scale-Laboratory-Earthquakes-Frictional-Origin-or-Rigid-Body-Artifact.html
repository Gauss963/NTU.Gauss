Secondary Weakening in dm-Scale Laboratory Earthquakes: Frictional Origin or Rigid-Body Artifact?

Posted on 2026-02-15 · Gauss T. Chang · Chun-Yu Ke

A dm-scale PMMA fault experiment that examines whether the observed secondary weakening stage reflects frictional behavior or rigid-body-motion-induced apparatus unloading.

Affiliation: Department of Civil Engineering, National Taiwan University

Corresponding Author: R14521220@ntu.edu.tw

Keywords: Laboratory Earthquakes, Earthquake Rupture Processes, LEFM, Cohesive zone model, PMMA fault

Abstract

Earthquakes arise from rapid slip along faults, and such dynamic processes are well described by linear elastic fracture mechanics (LEFM). In recent years, this framework has been extended to laboratory earthquakes in an effort to bridge laboratory and natural observations. However, laboratory and natural earthquakes differ by orders of magnitude in spatial scale and event size, and differences in boundary conditions further challenge the direct applicability of laboratory results to natural systems.

To investigate earthquake energy budgets under controlled conditions, we have developed a dm-scale single-direct-shear polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA) fault system consisting of a stationary and a moving block, subjected to independently controlled normal and shear loading. Normal stresses of 12-32 MPa are applied over a 50 cm x 5 cm fault area, while a constant shear loading rate is imposed on the moving block at the load point, promoting spontaneous rupture nucleation. A dense strain gauge array instrumented within 10 mm of the fault enables direct measurement of dynamic strain during rupture nucleation, propagation, and arrest, as well as estimation of on-fault weakening behavior. Preliminary results reveal a secondary weakening stage following the initial friction drop, with significantly larger amplitude that substantially increases apparent breakdown work and modifies source-time functions and far-field seismic radiation.

To interpret experimental observations, we perform finite-element simulations that explicitly incorporate the experimental geometry, elastic properties, and boundary conditions. Comparisons between numerical and measured strain fields allow us to examine rupture dynamics, energy localization, and the partitioning between fault dissipation and rigid-body-motion-induced apparatus unloading, and to assess the extent to which laboratory earthquakes satisfy LEFM assumptions, identifying deviations arising from finite-size effects.

While secondary weakening may be attributed to complex frictional constitutive behavior, our results highlight that energy release associated with rigid-body motion of the experimental blocks and apparatus unloading, a mechanism with no obvious direct analogue in natural earthquakes, plays a key role. As a consequence, energy release in laboratory earthquakes is often spatially non-localized, in contrast to the highly localized rupture processes assumed in LEFM descriptions of natural events.
